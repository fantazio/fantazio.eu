The action took place from February 24 to June 5, 2026.\
I was granted 7k€ on February 24 by the [OCaml Software Foundation](https://ocaml-sf.org)
to work on the [dead_code_analyzer](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer).
Following their standard rate of 70€/h, this amounts to 100h of work.

The selected points of interests were:
- ensuring the tool remains compatible with recent versions (and future) of OCaml,
- let me maintain the project freely,
- test the tool on existing projects.

The foundation's grant contributed to the development of
[releases 1.1.1 and 1.2.0](https://discuss.ocaml.org/t/ann-dead-code-analyzer-1-1-1-and-1-2-0/18164).

Over the action period, I spent a total of 152h on the project, of which
I invoiced 100h to the foundation.\
The following sections break down the work funded.
They are ordered by volume of work rather than chronologically.

## Documentation work (39.5h)

This work can be included in the version-compatibility and the
free-maintenance categories.\
The former because it helped discover and fix bugs causing different results
among different versions of the analyzer.
The latter because it also introduced bug fixes and is only indirectly related with the former.

Before this work, the existing documentation was focused on the general usage of
the tool.\
Now, it also describes each report section in more details, providing usage,
examples, and limitations.\
The analyzer's documentation can be found [here](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/tree/master/docs).

Most of this work is related to the meta-[issue #42](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/42).\
Doing this work helped define clearer semantics and uncovered issues
[#57](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/57),
[#64](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/64),
[#66](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/66),
and [#67](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/67).
Issue [#57](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/57) was fixed
during the process by [PR #65](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/pull/65).

## Study of the analyzer on opam (37h)

This is directly related to testing the tool on existing projects.

The suggested projects were [Dune](https://github.com/ocaml/dune),
[opam](https://github.com/ocaml/opam), and
[Rocq](https://github.com/rocq-prover/rocq).
The request was to study the results and provide feedback to the maintainers.
The hope was that it could provide visibility to the tool, encourage others to
use it and maybe find some existing limitations of the tool.\
I focused on a study of the dead_code_analyzer on opam and reinterpreted the
request and hope into 2 goals:
- find limitations of the analyzer,
- audit opam.

A study report is [available](https://fantazio.eu/reports/dca_opam/), and
the audit resulted in [PR #6954](https://github.com/ocaml/opam/pull/6954),
waiting for reviews.\
A key information of that report is that the **estimated fix rate** (before review)
is **71.9%** on opam.
The tool reaches a **precision of 92.3%** (99.8% on exported values), and 4 new
limitations were discovered during this study (issues
[#79](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/79),
[#80](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/80),
[#81](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/81),
and [#82](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/issues/82)).
The only false positive in the reported exported values is due to a
[documented limitation](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/blob/1.2.0/docs/exported_values/EXPORTED_VALUES.md#include-module-type-with-substitution)

### Future

The new issues will be addressed. If not fixed, they will at least be included
in the documentation.\
The PR is open and I intend to support the maintainers in their review. I also
plan to update the study report with additional observations and results
related to the PR, once it is closed.\
I would like to develop more automation to do the most repetitive cleanup taks.
An "auto-cleaner" tool could also benefit other codebase refactoring tasks.

## OCaml 5.3 compatibility (17h)

This is direclty related to ensuring compatibility with recent versions of OCaml.

The OCaml 5.3 compatibility work was already in progress before the start of the action.
[PR #38](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/pull/38) documents the changes
implemented to move from OCaml 5.2 to OCaml 5.3.

The main difficulty with updating to OCaml 5.3 was related to `Cmt_format.cmt_infos`.
The field `cmt_value_dependencies` that the analyzer relied on was replaced in OCaml 5.3 by `cmt_declaration_dependencies`.
Most of the work was focused on trying to reproduce this info
(via `cmt_declaration_dependencies` and `cmt_uid_to_decl`)
without impacting the performance of the tool too heavily (via a caching mechanism).\
As a result, dead_code_analyzer versions 1.1.1 and 1.2.0 take a **comparable
amount of time** to analyze [Frama-C](https://git.frama-c.com/pub/frama-c) but
the latter **consumes ~60% more memory** than the former (by default). A new
option is introduced to give user control of the internal cache size.

I suggested the implemented solution to
[reanalyze](https://github.com/rescript-lang/reanalyze) maintainers
([here](https://github.com/rescript-lang/reanalyze/pull/203#issuecomment-4422562964))
because this project took inspiration in the dead_code_analyzer. Although they
confirmed it works for them, I have no idea if they will move further with
the project.

### Future

The next target version is OCaml 5.4.\
Ideally, I would like to provide support for multiple OCaml versions
almost-transparently via an external lib.
The library would take care of the compatibility issues in the `Typedtree`
(and other places that changes in-between OCaml versions) and be the
intermediate between the analyzer and compiler-libs for most iteractions.
Such a library could potentially benefit other `Typedtree` users.

## OCaml 4.14 compatibility (2.5h)

This is direclty related to ensuring compatibility with recent versions of OCaml.
This work is [available on my fork](https://github.com/fantazio/dead_code_analyzer/tree/4.14).

Updating to this version was surprisingly simple. However, this is not tested
further than the analyzer's testsuite.

### Future

There is no intent to maintain it actively but rather opportunistically.
I.e. the branch will be updated upon new releases or user requests (issues or PRs).\
This version of the tool may be published in opam in the future,
[as suggested](https://discuss.ocaml.org/t/ann-dead-code-analyzer-1-1-1-and-1-2-0/18164/2?u=fantazio).\
Following the idea of providing support for multiple OCaml versions at once
via a lib, OCaml 4.14 would be included in releases.

## General maintenance (4h)

This is directly related to maintaing the tool freely.

This includes, disabling LexiFi's extension by default
([PR #76](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/pull/76)).
The option `--lexifi` is introduced to enable the extension.\
This also includes a small clean up of the root md files
([PR #75](https://github.com/LexiFi/dead_code_analyzer/pull/75)),
and release-related work.
